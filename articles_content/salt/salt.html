What is salt?

Salt is a mineral made almost exclusively (97-99%) of sodium chloride. In cooking, is it used as a flavor enhancer and all flavors, sweet ones included, benefit from it (Robuchon et al., 2017). Salt is transparent, usually appearing white. Any different color suggests the presence of minerals, for instance iron oxide in himalayan pink salt (Andersen & Orwig, 2026).

However, the tiny amount of minerals, especially when coupled with the small amount of salt used when cooking, does not affect taste: therefore salt should be odorless and not taste of anything besides saltiness (Chlebowski, 2024). The contrary may indicate problems with the ingredient.

- pure sodium chloride (Robuchon et al., 2017), meaning a mineral - Salt is a flavor enhancer, and everything, sweet things included, will benefit from it. - odorless (Chlebowski, 2024) (maybe other sou rce here?) -> does not add a taste, only an enhancer - white/colorless -> color = added mineral, e.g. grey, pink (himalayan, from iron oxyde (Andersen & Orwig, 2026))

 

How does it affect taste?

Besides salt, we can only perceive four other tastes: sour, bitter, sweet and umami (CookWell, n.d.), which we can combine with aromas perceived by our nose to achieve different flavors (SOURCE). Salt reinforces sweetness, sourness and umami while mellowing bitter tastes (Nosrat, 2017). “Salt also reduces our perception of bitterness, with the secondary effect of emphasising other flavours present in bitter dishes. Salt enhances sweetness while reducing bitterness in foods that are both bitter and sweet, such as bittersweet chocolate, coffee ice cream, or burnt caramels.” (Nosrat, 2017)

 

What types of salt are there? How are they different from one another? When to use each?

The majority of the salt we consume is sea salt, which has been obtained by evaporating ocean water. It is possible to consume some rock salts i.e. salts mined from deposits in caves, dead lakes and seas (Nosrat, 2017). While they usually contain many impurities (Danilo, 2023), some of them can be processed to be food safe, such as Himalayan salt. In cooking, the most important difference is in the size of the crystals and their density in sodium chloride. There are two broad categories that can be used to classify all salt types: Fine salt, also named table salt: Very small grains Highly concentrated in sodium Useful in low water environments, e.g. typically used when baking because of how quickly the grains dissolve thanks to their size Harder to store without caking; some fine salts are therefore sold with anti-caking agents, which might make soups a little cloudy (Farrimond, 2017), or it can be stored by putting grains of rice in the salt to absorb humidity Corse flaky salt, also sometimes referred to as kosher salt: Useful to visualize the amount on your food before the grains dissolve (Ragusea, 2021) Easier to grab with one’s fingers (Ragusea, 2021) Best for finishing touches and adding texture Since commercialized salts are almost exclusively made of sodium chloride, the type of salt you pick won’t directly affect taste (Chlebowski, 2024). Picking a favorite salt is a matter of personal preference and getting to know a chosen type/brand to become familiar with how concentrated the salt is. It is nevertheless good to have at least one fine salt and one crystal salt at home to be able to adapt the salt type to the water content of the food or being able to add an extra texture to a dish. Two additives that can be found in salt and might impact one’s decision when purchasing are:

  • Iodine, which should be sought by people who have an iodine deficiency (more information at the end of the article, health section)
  • Sodium nitrite, which prevents the growth of some harmful bacteria and is often found in salts sold as curing salts (Farrimond, 2017)
